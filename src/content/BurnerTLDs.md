# Hunting the Burner Phones of the Internet: Catching C2/Malware domains with DNS Deltas and Statistical Analysis

If you spend enough time playing with EDR telemetry, we all can agree how overwhelming it gets while diving and hunting through raw DNS logs. It’s like trying to find a needle in a haystack—while the haystack is actively on fire. 
The sheer volume of legitimate cloud telemetry, CDN routing, and background app chatter make it incredibly difficult to spot the single malicious Command and Control (C2) beacon hidden in the noise.

But threat actors have operational constraints, and those constraints leave predictable fingerprints. When standing up disposable infrastructure, they tend to favor what could easily be considered the "burner phones" of the internet: Domain extensions (TLDs) that are cheap, unvetted, and easy to automate.
As a threat hunter, I sometimes like to dedicate my hypothesis not around just TTPs but instead focus on operational constraints, psychology, and the mindset of threat actors. Sometimes combining these with TTPs could be very productive.

So here is a practical, step-by-step methodology for cutting through the noise to hunt down newly staged C2 infrastructure or malware hosting domains, and how we automate this entire workflow using the Delta Dtect platform.

## Step 1: Target the "Burner" TLDs

While sophisticated state-sponsored actors might compromise high-reputation `.com` or `.org` domains, commodity malware, ransomware affiliates, and initial access brokers often rely on cheap or free TLDs like `.xyz`, `.top`, `.info`, `.ru`, and `.cc`.

Focusing on these specific extensions dramatically reduces your initial dataset and increases the signal-to-noise ratio.

- **In Delta Dtect:** Navigate to the **DNS Delta Detection** module. The platform automatically organizes incoming telemetry by these high-risk TLDs. Simply select your target TLD from the navigation tabs to focus the investigation.

## Step 2: The Adya Analysis (Finding the Net-New)

Even within suspicious TLDs, looking at a single day's traffic isn't enough. Is this a strange but legitimate business application that always runs, or is this evidence of a fresh intrusion?

For the purpose of this analysis, we assume the attack occurred today and did not appear in the previous two days.

This is where the **[Adya Analysis](/blog/adya-hypothesis)** comes in. Instead of looking for "bad" things, we look for **"new" things**. We collect endpoint DNS logs from the past two days and mathematically subtract the baseline.

- **In Delta Dtect:** This process is handled automatically the moment you select a date. The backend dynamically shifts the baseline window, executes SentinelOne PowerQueries to retrieve the historical 48-hour data, compares it against today's traffic, and surfaces only the **net-new domains** that endpoints have never attempted to resolve before.

## Step 3: DGA and Entropy Analysis (Spotting the Math)

Now that we have a filtered list of net-new domains on suspicious TLDs, we need to determine whether they were generated by a human or a machine.

Modern malware heavily relies on **Domain Generation Algorithms (DGAs)** to rapidly rotate C2 endpoints. A human might register something like `update-server-v2.xyz`, while a DGA might generate something like `q8x2zp9k1.xyz`.

We can detect this mathematically using **Shannon Entropy**. High entropy indicates a high level of unpredictability, which often suggests machine-generated domain names.

- **In Delta Dtect:** Click the **Entropy Analysis** button on your dashboard. The platform's statistical engine calculates **Bivariate Z-Scores** for every domain in your net-new list. It measures both raw character entropy and domain length, comparing them against the **Median Absolute Deviation (MAD)** of the day's traffic. Any domain that spikes statistically is flagged as a **potential outlier**.


## Step 4: The WHOIS Reality Check

High entropy alone is not definitive proof of malicious activity. Sometimes legitimate technology companies use random-looking CDN or infrastructure strings.

To better determine malicious intent, we need to examine the **age of the domain**.

Legitimate infrastructure is usually registered months or years in advance. Disposable C2 infrastructure, however, is often registered just hours or days before a campaign begins.

- **In Delta Dtect:** Click the **Whois Reg Dates** button. The platform sequentially queries registration data for the anomalous domains and generates a **Newly Registered Domain (NRD) scatter plot**.

Because WHOIS queries can be slow, Delta Dtect converts the generated graph into a **Base64 string** and securely caches it in your local **PostgreSQL database**, allowing it to load instantly the next time the data is reviewed.

## Step 5: Correlation and the Kill Chain

This is where the hunt comes together. You now have two key pieces of evidence:

1. **Findings from Step 3:** A list of domains exhibiting high, machine-generated entropy.
2. **Findings from Step 4:** A visual timeline showing which of those domains were registered within the last 90 days.

When a domain shows a highly anomalous **Z-score** and was registered only a few days ago on a suspicious extension like `.top`, you are very likely observing **active adversary infrastructure**.
